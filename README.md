# Fate — Game Jam Case Study

## Overview

Fate is a classic action platformer with metroidvania elements, inspired by games like *Giana Sisters*, *Castlevania*, and *Mario*.

Developed by a team of 6 people during a 48-hour game jam in Plovdiv (2026).

---

## About the Game

*When something good happens to us, we call it fate.*  
*The same goes for the bad things in life.*  

This is the story of Isaac — on the day his child is born, a tragic accident occurs, and Death strikes a deal with him.

## Challenges

- **Forming the team** — finding the right people and building trust quickly  
- **Aligning ideas** — moving from multiple concepts (RPG, simulation) to a clear direction  
- **Technical differences** — adapting to different tools and workflows  
- **Time pressure** — delivering a working product within 48 hours  

Despite these challenges, we managed to build a playable prototype and present it successfully.

---

## What I Learned

- **Teamwork matters more than individual skill**  
- **Clear communication is critical under time pressure**  
- **Fast decision-making is essential in constrained environments**

## Full Development Story (Optional)

> This section describes the full experience of the game jam from my perspective.

The first challenge we encountered (even before the team was created) was finding the right people. There were certain criteria we had to meet in order to apply for the jam.

After creating the team "BEERЩАЙН" (*beershtain*), we faced the second challenge — we had the people, but not yet a real team. We decided to work on that first, because better no team than a broken one.

After a few hours, we got to know each other better and started brainstorming the game idea.

We moved from RPG to simulation and eventually settled on a platformer. Our goal was for everyone to be satisfied with the project, story, and visuals.

This is where we stopped on the first night. We decided to rest and meet again in the morning.

The next morning, with clear minds and a direction, we started building the game. We quickly realized it wouldn’t be easy — each of us was used to different technologies (even within the same field). This became our third challenge: choosing the right tools and learning new ones within just a few hours.

By the end of the day, we had a functional level with a somewhat playable and animated character, but we were still far from a complete project.

Some of us decided to stay overnight and continue working, while others rested. The next day, we swapped — the rested members took over.

We still didn’t have a presentation, a final boss, and many other elements, but we knew we had something working — an early version that could be shown.

This led to our fourth challenge — ourselves. Tired and exhausted, we had to finish everything in less than five hours.

In the end, we managed to deliver a working project and present it to the audience.
